The Federal Reserve held its benchmark interest rate steady at a target range of 5.25% to 5.50% at its June meeting, marking the seventh consecutive pause. The decision was unanimous among all twelve voting members of the Federal Open Market Committee.

In its accompanying statement, the FOMC noted that inflation has eased over the past year but remains elevated. The committee pointed to strong job gains and low unemployment as signs of continued economic resilience. The personal consumption expenditures price index, the Fed's preferred inflation gauge, rose 2.6% over the twelve months ending in May, down from a peak of 7.1% in June 2022 but still above the 2% target.

Fed Chair Jerome Powell said in a press conference that the committee wants to see more evidence that inflation is moving sustainably toward 2% before cutting rates. He described the current policy stance as "restrictive" and said the risks to the economic outlook have become more balanced. Powell noted that the labor market, while still strong, has shown signs of cooling, with the unemployment rate ticking up to 4.0% from 3.4% a year earlier.

Markets had largely priced in the hold decision. The CME FedWatch tool showed a 99.4% probability of no change heading into the meeting. Treasury yields were little changed following the announcement, with the 10-year note yielding 4.28%.
