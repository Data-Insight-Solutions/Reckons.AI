The common octopus (Octopus vulgaris) is a marine mollusk belonging to the class Cephalopoda. It is found in tropical and temperate waters worldwide, particularly in the Mediterranean Sea and the eastern Atlantic Ocean.

Octopuses have three hearts: two branchial hearts that pump blood through the gills, and one systemic heart that pumps blood through the body. Their blood is blue because it contains copper-based hemocyanin rather than iron-based hemoglobin. An adult common octopus typically weighs between 3 and 5 kilograms and has an arm span of up to 1 meter.

Octopuses are considered among the most intelligent invertebrates. They can solve mazes, open jars, and use tools. In laboratory experiments at the University of Otago in New Zealand, octopuses demonstrated the ability to recognize individual human faces. They have approximately 500 million neurons, with about two-thirds located in their arms rather than their central brain.

The octopus is a master of camouflage. Specialized skin cells called chromatophores allow them to change color and texture in less than a second. They use this ability for both hunting and predator avoidance. Their primary predators include moray eels, sharks, and dolphins.

Octopuses are solitary creatures with a short lifespan of 1 to 2 years. The female dies shortly after her eggs hatch, having spent weeks guarding them without eating. Males typically die within months of mating. This reproductive strategy is known as semelparity.

The common octopus primarily feeds on crabs, crayfish, and bivalve mollusks. It hunts mainly at night, using its keen eyesight and sensitive suckers to locate prey. Each arm has approximately 240 suckers that can taste and grip independently.
